Predicting Heart Disease with Machine Learning¶

Introduction: The Problem¶

Cardiovascular disease remains the leading cause of death globally. Early detection can save lives, but identifying individuals at risk can require costly or invasive tests.

The overarching question I am to answer is, "Can we use patient health indicators to predict the likelihood of heart disease?"

For this project, I will:

  1. Build a classification model that predicts whether a patient has heart disease (1) or not (0).
  2. Explore which factors (e.g., age, cholesterol, chest pain) are most predictive.
  3. Compare multiple classification algorithms to determine which performs best.

Data Introduction¶

I am using the Heart Disease Dataset from Kaggle: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/johnsmith88/heart-disease-dataset

Features:

  • age: Patient’s age (years)
  • sex: 1 = male, 0 = female
  • cp: Chest pain type (0–3, indicating increasing severity)
  • trestbps: Resting blood pressure (mm Hg)
  • chol: Serum cholesterol (mg/dl)
  • fbs: Fasting blood sugar > 120 mg/dl (1 = true, 0 = false)
  • restecg: Resting electrocardiographic results (0–2)
  • thalach: Maximum heart rate achieved
  • exang: Exercise induced angina (1 = yes, 0 = no)
  • oldpeak: ST depression induced by exercise
  • slope: Slope of the peak exercise ST segment (0–2)
  • ca: Number of major vessels (0–3)
  • thal: Thalassemia (0–3)
  • target: 1 = heart disease, 0 = no heart disease

This dataset has 303 rows × 14 columns.

Maximum Heart Rate (thalach) - Lower achievable heart rates often indicate reduced cardiac function.

Thalassemia (thal) and Age (age) also provide moderate predictive relative importance, reflecting genetic and age related factors.

My Interpretation of visual:

In contrast, baseline metrics like sex, fasting blood sugar (fbs), and resting ECG (restecg) contribute less individually but are still important to track and include to support the model’s fine-tuning.

Overall Takeaway of Visual: The Random Forest results reinforce that heart disease is primarily signaled by symptom based and physiological responses rather than just simple demographic variables. This combination of interpretability and predictive accuracy makes tree-based models particularly effective in healthcare data analysis.

# I'll train and compare multiple classification models to predict heart disease presence

from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.neighbors import KNeighborsClassifier
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score, precision_score, recall_score, f1_score, roc_auc_score

# Each model represents a different learning approach (linear, distance-based, and ensemble)
models = {
    "Logistic Regression": LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000, random_state=42), 
    "K-Nearest Neighbors": KNeighborsClassifier(n_neighbors=5),                
    "Random Forest": RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=42) 
}

# Store each model's performance metrics for comparison
results = []

for name, model in models.items():
    model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    y_pred = model.predict(X_test)
    
    # Compute the key evaluation metrics here 
    results.append({
        "Model": name,
        "Accuracy": accuracy_score(y_test, y_pred),
        "Precision": precision_score(y_test, y_pred),
        "Recall": recall_score(y_test, y_pred),
        "F1 Score": f1_score(y_test, y_pred),
        "ROC-AUC": roc_auc_score(y_test, y_pred)
    })

# Sorting by F1 Score provides a balanced view of model performance and prints
results_df = pd.DataFrame(results).sort_values(by="F1 Score", ascending=False)
results_df.reset_index(drop=True, inplace=True)
results_df

Modeling¶

To predict the presence of heart disease, I used three classification algorithms:

1️. Logistic Regression¶

"A linear model that estimates probabilities using the logistic function."

  • Pros: Interpretable and simple, but also efficient on smaller datasets.
  • Cons: Does assume linear relationships and can underperform with complex data, which is non-linear data. (In this case = diffuclt).

2️. K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)¶

"A non-parametric model that classifies based on the majority vote of the k nearest samples in feature space."

  • Pros: Simple and no assumptions about data distribution.
  • Cons: Sensitive to scaling and large datasets; can struggle with high-dimensional data. (Not using high dimensional data in this at the moment).

3️. Random Forest¶

"An ensemble method that builds multiple decision trees and averages their predictions to improve accuracy and reduce overfitting."

  • Pros: Handles both linear and non-linear data well and does provide feature importance.
  • Cons: Less interpretable and more computationally in depth!

By comparing these models, I aimed to balance accuracy, interpretability, and robustness for medical prediction for my given questions! :)

Evaluation¶

To evaluate model performance, I used accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC.

Why Did I Choose These Metrics?¶

  • Accuracy: Overall correctness of the model.
  • Precision: How many predicted positives were actually correct.
  • Recall: How many actual positives were correctly identified.
  • F1 Score: Balances precision and recall. This is ideal for medical diagnosis where both errors matter.
  • ROC-AUC: Measures how well the model separates classes overall.

Results Summary:¶

  • Random Forest achieved the highest overall performance across all metrics, especially F1 and ROC-AUC, meaning it’s best at distinguishing between heart disease and healthy cases.
  • Logistic Regression performed solidly and definitley offering interpretability with moderate performance.
  • KNN was less consistent. Struggling with overlapping feature distributions.

Conclusion:
The Random Forest classifier provides the most reliable and accurate predictions for heart disease in this dataset thus, making it the optimal model for this problem.

Ethical and Social Impact¶

While predictive modeling in healthcare offers great promise there is defintley going to be challanges, just like with anything:

  • Ethical Risks: Misclassifications can have serious consequences. These false negatives may delay treatment, while false positives may cause unnecessary anxiety or procedures.
  • Bias Considerations: The dataset may not represent all populations equally (e.g., age, gender, ethnicity). This could lead to unequal or incorrect model performance across demographic groups.
  • Data Privacy: Medical data must be protected and used responsibly.
  • Social Impact: Early prediction can save lives by identifying at risk individuals sooner. It supports preventive healthcare and efficient resource allocation.

Reflection:
Models like this should be used as support tools AND not replacements for doctors. The goal is to assist clinical judgment and not override it. Ethical use can help ensure these technologies enhance healthcare without introducing bias or harm.
